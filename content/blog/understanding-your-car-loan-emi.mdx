Buying a car is exciting — but the financing decision behind it often gets far
less attention than the colour or the alloy wheels. After 15+ years arranging
vehicle finance, I can tell you that understanding your **EMI** is the single
most valuable thing a buyer can do before signing.

## What is an EMI?

EMI stands for **Equated Monthly Installment** — the fixed amount you pay the
lender every month until the loan is fully repaid. Each EMI is made up of two
parts:

- **Principal** — the portion that reduces your actual loan balance.
- **Interest** — the lender's charge for lending you the money.

In the early months, most of your EMI goes toward interest. As the balance
shrinks, more of each payment chips away at the principal.

## The formula behind it

The EMI is calculated as:

> EMI = P × r × (1 + r)ⁿ / ((1 + r)ⁿ − 1)

Where **P** is the loan amount, **r** is the monthly interest rate (annual rate
÷ 12 ÷ 100), and **n** is the number of monthly installments.

You don't need to do this by hand — try the [EMI calculator](/tools) on this
site and adjust the sliders to see how the numbers move in real time.

## Three levers that change your EMI

| Lever               | Effect on EMI | Effect on total interest |
| ------------------- | ------------- | ------------------------ |
| Larger down payment | Lower         | Lower                    |
| Longer tenure       | Lower         | **Higher**               |
| Lower interest rate | Lower         | Lower                    |

The trap most buyers fall into is stretching the **tenure** to shrink the
monthly number. It feels cheaper, but you end up paying far more interest
overall.

## My practical advice

1. **Keep the tenure as short as your budget allows.** A 3-year loan costs
   dramatically less than a 7-year loan on the same car.
2. **Negotiate the rate, not just the price.** Even 0.5% makes a real
   difference over the life of the loan.
3. **Check the on-road, not just ex-showroom.** Insurance and processing fees
   are often financeable — know what you're actually borrowing.
